The Problem We're Solving

Journalism has mastered telling people what's wrong.
It rarely tells them where to go next.

A reader finishes a story about a utility rate hike, a data center threatening local water supplies, or an air quality violation that went unpunished. They're informed. They may even be outraged. And then they close the tab — because nothing in the story told them there was a public hearing next Tuesday, a comment deadline on Friday, or an elected official whose phone number is public record.

That gap isn't a minor inconvenience. It's a democratic failure. Research consistently shows that civic knowledge is the single strongest predictor of civic participation — people don't disengage because they don't care, they disengage because no one handed them a pathway. When Planet Detroit started pairing accountability stories with plain-language guides to participation, readers showed up to township meetings and filed regulatory comments for the first time in their lives. They told us: "I didn't know I could do this."

First Tool from the Lab

Civic Action Builder

Local journalism tells readers what's happening. The Civic Action Builder helps them understand what they can do about it.

Using AI, the tool analyzes published articles and automatically generates civic engagement resources — relevant public meetings, comment deadlines, elected officials, and local organizations — directly embedded in the story. Editors review everything before it publishes. AI assists; journalists decide.

We are currently piloting the Civic Action Builder with a select group of community newsrooms across the country, with more partners joining in 2026. Our work has attracted early recognition from national journalism innovation funders.

What once took a reporter 30–60 minutes of manual research now takes 5 minutes of editorial review.

What's Next

Tools in development

The Civic Action Builder is the first tool out of the lab — not the last. We're actively developing and exploring a pipeline of tools designed to address the most persistent resource constraints facing community newsrooms.

In Development

Ask Planet Detroit

A semantic search tool that lets readers find relevant past coverage by meaning, not just keywords — so "Is my tap water safe?" surfaces everything a newsroom has published on the topic, regardless of how it was worded.

In Development

Organization Directory

A structured, searchable database of civic and advocacy organizations — tagged by issue area and geography — that newsrooms can embed in their coverage and readers can use to find local points of connection.

Exploring

Accountability Data Trackers

Automated tools that aggregate and visualize public enforcement, compliance, and permitting data — making regulatory activity visible and understandable for reporters and readers who don't have time to chase raw databases.

Exploring

Civic Digest Generator

Auto-generated roundups of upcoming public meetings, comment deadlines, and civic opportunities — pulled from a newsroom's coverage areas and delivered as a reader-ready newsletter or standalone page.

Tools labeled "In Development" are actively being built and tested at Planet Detroit. Tools labeled "Exploring" are in early research and scoping phases.

About PD Civic Action Lab

Journalism experience.
Civic infrastructure expertise.
Working prototypes.

Planet Detroit is an independent nonprofit environmental newsroom founded in 2019, operating with a small, dedicated team. We have earned recognition for health and environmental reporting, engagement journalism, and business sustainability from SPJ Detroit, INN, and LION Publishers.

The PD Civic Action Lab grows from that foundation. Our founder spent 15 years as a community planner for Oakland County before building Planet Detroit — giving the lab an unusually deep understanding of how civic infrastructure actually works, and what information residents need to use it. The Civic Action Builder is not a prototype awaiting real-world testing. It is already deployed, already in use, and already helping readers show up.

Planet Detroit has received institutional support from the MacArthur Foundation, Kresge Foundation, Joyce Foundation, Solutions Journalism Network, and Press Forward.
